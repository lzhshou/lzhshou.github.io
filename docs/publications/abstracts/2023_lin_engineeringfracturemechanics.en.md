## Introduction
### Research Background and Significance

The mechanical and fracturing behaviors of geo-materials, such as rocks and concrete, are significantly influenced by the presence of stochastic inclusions, which are naturally occurring heterogeneities like voids, cracks, or mineral grains. Understanding these behaviors is critical for applications in civil engineering, mining, and geotechnical engineering, where accurate predictions of material failure are essential for safety and design optimization. Traditional numerical methods often struggle to capture the complex interactions between the matrix material and inclusions due to their inherent randomness and variability. This study addresses this gap by proposing a novel approach that combines the Finite-Discrete Element Method (FDEM) with a stochastic reconstruction strategy to model the mechanical and fracturing responses of geo-materials with stochastic inclusions. The research aims to provide a more realistic and reliable framework for predicting material failure under various loading conditions. 

### Main Research Methods

The study employs the Finite-Discrete Element Method (FDEM), a hybrid numerical technique that integrates the strengths of the Finite Element Method (FEM) for continuum mechanics and the Discrete Element Method (DEM) for discontinuum mechanics. This allows for the simulation of both elastic deformation and crack propagation within the material. To account for the stochastic nature of inclusions, a novel reconstruction strategy is introduced. This strategy uses statistical data from real-world geo-materials to generate synthetic microstructures that accurately reflect the size, shape, and distribution of inclusions. The reconstructed models are then subjected to various mechanical loading scenarios, including uniaxial compression, tension, and shear, to study their mechanical responses and fracturing patterns. Advanced algorithms are implemented to ensure computational efficiency while maintaining high accuracy in capturing the complex interactions between the matrix and inclusions. 

### Key Findings and Conclusions

The results demonstrate that the presence of stochastic inclusions significantly alters the mechanical and fracturing behaviors of geo-materials. Specifically, inclusions act as stress concentrators, leading to localized crack initiation and propagation. The study reveals that the spatial distribution and geometric characteristics of inclusions play a crucial role in determining the overall failure mode and strength of the material. For instance, materials with densely packed inclusions exhibit lower tensile strength but higher energy dissipation during fracture compared to those with sparse inclusions. The FDEM approach, combined with the stochastic reconstruction strategy, successfully captures these phenomena, providing a comprehensive understanding of the material's response under different loading conditions. The findings highlight the importance of considering stochastic inclusions in the design and analysis of geo-materials to ensure structural integrity and safety. 

### Innovation Points and Application Value

The primary innovation of this study lies in the integration of FDEM with a stochastic reconstruction strategy, which offers a more realistic representation of geo-materials with inherent heterogeneities. This approach overcomes the limitations of traditional methods that often rely on simplified or idealized models. The proposed framework is highly versatile and can be applied to a wide range of geo-materials, including rocks, concrete, and ceramics, making it a valuable tool for both academic research and industrial applications. The insights gained from this study can inform the design of more resilient structures, improve risk assessment in geotechnical engineering, and enhance the efficiency of mining operations. Furthermore, the methodology can be extended to other fields, such as materials science and biomechanics, where the understanding of heterogeneous material behavior is equally critical. Overall, this research contributes to advancing the state-of-the-art in fracture mechanics and provides a robust foundation for future studies on complex material systems.
